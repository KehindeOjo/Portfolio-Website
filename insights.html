Dashboards & BI

Why Most Dashboards Fail to Drive Decisions

By Kehinde Ojo  ·  Data Analyst

Walk into most organizations today and you’ll find dashboards everywhere. Screens, reports, and slides packed with charts, KPIs, and colorful visualizations. And yet, decisions are still made on gut feeling. Meetings still turn into debates about which number to trust. The data is there, the dashboards are there, so why aren’t they working?

After building dashboards across financial, operational, and HR contexts, I've noticed the failure is rarely about the data quality or the tools. The failure is almost always about design intent.

"A dashboard full of charts is not the same thing as a dashboard that changes behaviour."

1. They answer the wrong question

Most dashboards are built to show everything: revenue, expenses, headcount, conversion rates, stock levels, all on a single page. The analyst behind it worked hard, but the executive looking at it doesn’t know what to do next. A good dashboard answers one primary question: what decision does this person need to make, and what do they need to see to make it confidently? Everything else is noise.

When I built the Estate Financial Tracker, the primary question was simple: which units are generating revenue and which aren't? Every visual on that dashboard pointed at that question. Nothing else made the cut.

2. They show data, not direction

There’s a difference between a number and a signal. Showing that revenue this month is ₦4.2M tells you a fact. Showing that it’s down 12% from last month, that the decline started in week two, and that it’s concentrated in one billing category tells you where to look. Most dashboards stop at the fact. High-impact dashboards surface the signal.

This is why KPI frameworks matter so much. A raw metric without a target, trend, or comparison is decorative. The moment you add context such as a benchmark, prior period, or threshold, the metric becomes a decision trigger.

3. They're built for the analyst, not the audience

Analysts love detail. We want to see the granular breakdown, the drill-through, the underlying table. So we build dashboards that reflect that. But the CFO reviewing it at 8am before a board meeting needs three things: is this good or bad, why, and what should we do? If they have to hunt for that answer, the dashboard has already failed.

The fix is to design backwards. Start with the audience, their decision, and their available time. Then build the visuals that serve that. Put the headline metric first, the supporting context second, and the detailed drill-down available but not prominent.

The dashboard that works

A dashboard that drives decisions is opinionated. It has a point of view. It tells the viewer what matters and what doesn’t. It is built for a specific person making a specific decision, not for every possible user who might ever log in. That level of intentionality is what separates a reporting tool from a decision-making tool, and it is the standard I hold every visualization I build to.

Process Automation

How Automation Changed My Approach to Financial Reporting

By Kehinde Ojo  ·  Data Analyst

There was a period in my work where a significant portion of every week went to the same tasks: pulling transaction data, formatting it, running reconciliations, and assembling reports that looked identical to the ones from the week before. It was careful work. It was necessary work. But it was also work that a well-designed system could do without me.

When I built my first automated reporting workflow using Excel, Power Query, and later Python, I didn’t just save time. I changed the way I thought about what my job actually was.

"Automation didn't replace the analyst. It clarified what the analyst should actually be doing."

The shift from production to interpretation

Before automation, most of my time was spent producing the report. After automation, the report produced itself. That freed up something more valuable than time: it freed up attention. I could focus on what the numbers actually meant. Why payroll costs spiked in March. What was driving the variance between projected and actual revenue. Which expense categories were growing faster than the business they were meant to support.

These are the questions that create value. They require judgment, context, and curiosity, things a script cannot provide. Automation pushed me toward those questions by removing the manual work that had been crowding them out.

Errors dropped. Trust increased.

Manual financial reporting is error prone by nature, not because analysts are careless, but because copying, pasting, and reformatting data at volume under time pressure introduces mistakes: a figure entered in the wrong cell, a formula that did not extend to the last row, or prior month data accidentally carried forward.

Automated workflows are consistent. They apply the same logic to the same data in the same sequence every time. In my experience, this reliability does something important beyond accuracy: it builds trust. When stakeholders know that the report they are looking at was generated from a clean, repeatable process rather than assembled by hand at 11pm, they engage with it differently. They use it to make decisions rather than questioning its accuracy.

What automation actually requires

There’s a misconception that automation is purely a technical skill. Learn Python or Apps Script and suddenly everything becomes automatic. In practice, building a reliable automated reporting workflow requires something more fundamental: a precise understanding of the process you are automating. You have to know where the data comes from, what transformations it needs, what exceptions exist, and what the output must look like. If that process knowledge is not there, you do not automate a workflow, you automate a mess.

This is why I believe financial and accounting experience is an underrated foundation for automation work. Understanding the logic of reconciliation, the structure of financial statements, and why certain figures move together is what allows you to build automation that is genuinely reliable rather than just technically functional.

The mindset that stays with me

Every time I encounter a repetitive financial task now, my first instinct is to ask: what is the underlying logic here, and can it be systematized? Not every process should be automated, some require human judgment at every step. But asking this question consistently keeps me oriented toward building things that scale, rather than doing things that do not.

Financial Analysis

3 Things Financial Data Reveals That Spreadsheets Hide

By Kehinde Ojo  ·  Data Analyst

Spreadsheets are one of the most powerful financial tools ever built. I use them constantly for modeling, reconciliation, tracking, and reporting. But there is a specific way most people use spreadsheets that actively conceals the most valuable information in their financial data. It is not a problem with the tool. It is a problem with the frame.

When financial data lives only in rows and columns, certain patterns become invisible, not because the data is missing, but because the structure of the spreadsheet is not designed to surface them. Here are three things I consistently find once data is properly analyzed.

  • 1

    The 20% driving 80% of the problem

    In almost every financial dataset I’ve worked with, a small number of transactions, accounts, or cost centres are responsible for a disproportionate share of variance, loss, or inefficiency. This is the Pareto principle in financial form, and it is almost always invisible in a flat spreadsheet view.

    When I analyzed financial data during audit work, it was not the total figures that revealed the ₦5M discrepancy, but drilling into the distribution of transactions and identifying the specific accounts where patterns broke down. A summary row in a spreadsheet would have masked that entirely. It took disaggregation and pattern analysis to surface it.

    This is why analytical tools that support segmentation and distribution analysis such as Power BI, Python, and SQL reveal things that spreadsheet totals cannot. Totals summarize. Analysis reveals.

  • 2

    Trends that only appear over time

    A spreadsheet snapshot shows you where you are. It rarely shows you the direction you are moving in, or how fast. A cost that looks acceptable in isolation may be growing at 15% month on month. A revenue stream that appears stable may have been declining gradually for six months. These trends are invisible in a single period view.

    The most useful financial analyses I've done have involved taking what looked like stable data and plotting it across time. Suddenly a flat-looking expense line shows a clear seasonal spike. A reliable revenue source shows early signs of compression. A department's overtime costs start growing three months before their headcount request lands on the table.

    "A number tells you where you are. A trend tells you where you're going — and whether you should be worried."

    Spreadsheets can produce trend charts, but most financial spreadsheets aren't structured to do this easily. The data sits in columns that require significant restructuring before time-series analysis is possible. This is one of the clearest cases where moving data into a proper analytical environment pays off immediately.

  • 3

    The relationship between variables that aren't on the same sheet

    Financial data rarely tells its full story in one table. Headcount and payroll costs live in different sheets. Revenue and sales activity live in different systems. Inventory levels and procurement spend are tracked in separate files. In a traditional spreadsheet setup, these connections are manual, someone copies a number from one file into another, and the relationship between the two datasets is never formally modeled.

    Some of the most valuable insights I have surfaced came from connecting datasets that were never analyzed together. Linking operational efficiency data to cost data revealed which process improvements actually translated to financial savings. Connecting HR attrition data to department performance data showed which teams were understaffed relative to their output expectations.

    SQL, Power BI relationships, and Python’s data merging capabilities make these connections explicit and queryable. The insight is not in any one table, it is in the join between them. Spreadsheets, used in isolation, simply cannot show you that.

The spreadsheet isn't the problem

None of this is an argument against spreadsheets. They are excellent for what they are designed to do: structured calculation, financial modeling, and organized record keeping. The limitation is not the tool, but treating the spreadsheet as the final step in the analytical process rather than the starting point.

The financial data your organization generates contains patterns, signals, and relationships that have real operational and strategic value. The question is whether your current tools and processes are set up to surface them, or whether the most important information is quietly hiding in a column that nobody has thought to analyze yet.
